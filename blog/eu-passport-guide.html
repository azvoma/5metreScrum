EU Passport Eligibility: The Complete Guide for Rugby Players in 2026

5 Metre Scrum Editorial · 8 min read · Updated July 2026

Ask any agent what single line on a rugby CV most changes a player's market, and the answer is rarely a bench press number. It's a passport. A player who counts as "non-foreign" for roster purposes in France, England or the URC is simply easier to sign — and clubs pay for easy.

Why passports matter to clubs

Almost every major league restricts how many "foreign" players a club can field or register. In France, the JIFF system rewards clubs for fielding players developed in the French system and effectively caps non-JIFF signings. English and URC clubs juggle overseas quotas of their own. A player holding an EU or relevant national passport can slot into a roster without consuming one of those precious foreign spots — which means more clubs can bid, and bidding is what raises value.

The main eligibility routes

1. Citizenship by descent

The most common route for Southern Hemisphere players. Ireland allows citizenship through an Irish-born grandparent (via the Foreign Births Register). Italy has historically recognised descent through the paternal line across multiple generations, though rules tightened recently — check the current decree position. Portugal, Poland, Lithuania and Germany all have ancestry routes with different documentation thresholds.

2. Residency

Living and playing in an EU country for a qualifying period (typically 5 years, sometimes reduced) can open naturalisation. Some players effectively "qualify while playing" on development contracts.

3. Marriage or family reunification

Slower and more scrutinised, but real. Timelines vary hugely by country.

Paperwork reality check: ancestry claims live or die on documents — long-form birth certificates, marriage certificates, and apostilles across two or three generations. Start collecting before you need them. A claim that takes 18 months to process is worthless for a contract that starts in August.

What to put on your rugby CV

  • Held passports — list every one, current and valid.
  • Eligibility in progress — if you have a live application, say so, with a realistic timeline.
  • Potential eligibility — a documented Irish grandparent is worth flagging even before you apply; a good agent or club will help you finish the process.

Common mistakes

Don't claim eligibility you can't document — clubs verify, and a withdrawn claim ends conversations. Don't confuse national-team eligibility (World Rugby regulations) with employment eligibility (passports and visas) — they're separate systems. And don't sit on a valid ancestry claim because the forms look tedious; it may be the highest-return admin work of your career.
