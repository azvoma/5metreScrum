Deadlift Standards for Rugby Positions: What Numbers Actually Matter to Scouts

By 5 Metre Scrum Strength & Conditioning Team · 6 min read · Updated 28 June 2026

Content prepared with reference to published strength and conditioning standards and combine data from verified platform profiles.

The deadlift is rugby's favourite gym number for a reason: it's the lift that best expresses total-body force through the hips and posterior chain — the exact engine behind scrummaging, cleaning out and breaking tackles. But raw numbers without context mislead, and scouts know it. World Rugby's own strength and conditioning guidance backs this up as a core marker of on-field force production.

Absolute benchmarks by position

Position groupSemi-pro solidPro standardElite
Props220kg250kg280kg+
Hookers / locks210kg235kg260kg+
Back row200kg225kg250kg+
Centres190kg210kg235kg+
Back three / halves170kg195kg220kg+

Relative strength is the smarter number

Recruiters increasingly look at deadlift-to-bodyweight ratio, because it predicts on-field expression better than the absolute lift. Rough guide: 2.0× bodyweight is a professional baseline for backs and loose forwards; 2.2×+ is genuinely strong at any level; tight-five players carrying heavy mass are often assessed closer to absolute numbers plus scrummaging outcomes.

How a scout reads "240kg deadlift": first question — verified how? Gym video with visible plates, or a coach-signed testing sheet, changes everything. Second question — at what bodyweight? Third — trap bar or straight bar? (Trap bar numbers run roughly 5–10% higher; say which you tested.)

Reporting it properly

  • State the bar type and whether it was a 1RM or calculated from reps.
  • Include bodyweight on the same date.
  • Video your top set — full lockout, plates visible. Thirty seconds of footage ends all arguments.
  • Retest each pre-season and update your profile; a dated number is a dead number.

Don't chase the number at the wrong cost

A deadlift that climbs while your Bronco slips is a trade most positions can't afford. Strength is a supporting stat: it gets read alongside speed and engine, never instead of them. The profile that signs is balanced — a 225kg pull next to a 4:30 Bronco beats a 260kg pull next to a 5:20 every time outside the front row.
